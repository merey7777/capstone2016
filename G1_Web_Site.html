Agua Fria National Monument
The Agua Fria National Monument and the surrounding area is a culturally significant place to the Yavapai people, with historical connection to the Hopi Tribe. The Monument contains more than 400 archaeological sites and was home to well over 3,000 ancestors at one time. It is located approximately 40 miles north of Phoenix, Arizona. The area is located on a high mesa semi-desert grassland, cut by the canyon of the Agua Fria River and other ribbons of valuable riparian forest, contributing to an outstanding biological resource. The diversity of vegetative communities, topographic features, and a dormant volcano decorates the landscape with a big rocky, basaltic plateau and offers great outdoor opportunities for observing cultural history, wildlife and scenic vistas.  Read more…

Bell Rock

Cameron Trading Post
The Cameron Trading Post is a historically significant place to the Hopi, Havasupai, Navajo and other Indigenous Peoples in Northern Arizona. It was established by two Anglo brothers in 1911 soon after a swayback suspension bridge was erected over the Little Colorado River allowing access over the gorge. Today, local natives continue to trade wool, blankets, artwork, jewelry and other items for goods or money. In the past, their grandparents use to be able to trade live stock at the post. Now the post offers a nice restaurant and convenient hotel to stay and eat. Read more…

Grand Canyon National Park
The Grand Canyon National Park is a culturally significant place and considered sacred by a number of Indigenous Peoples, including the Paiute, Havasupai, Hopi, Hualapai, Yavapai and Zuni. The hundreds of archaeological sites in the park have been linked to many of these Peoples. The Grand Canyon National Park borders 3 Indian reservations; lands of the Havasupai Tribe, the Hualapai Tribe and the Navajo Nation. The Grand Canyon National Park is the United States’ 15th oldest national park. Named a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1979, the park is located in northwestern Arizona. The park’s central feature is the Grand Canyon, a deep gorge carved from the meandering and powerful waters of the Colorado River, which is often considered one of the Seven Natural Wonders of the World. Read more…

Little Colorado River Gorge
The Little Colorado River Gorge is culturally significant and considered a sacred place to the Hopi people and other Indigenous Peoples. According to Hopi oral story, this is a place of Hopi emergence into this current fourth world. The Gorge is a spectacular canyon system cut from the main waters of the Little Colorado River which originates over 150 miles away from the springs of sacred Mount Baldy in the White Mountains. The 150 mile Little Colorado River valley is home to hundreds of old ancestral village sites (ruins) that dot the landscape. Located on the Navajo Nation reservation, the Little Colorado River Gorge is a great stopping point before continuing onward to the visit the majestic Grand Canyon just minutes away.  Read more…

Mingus Mountain
Mingus Mountain is a culturally significant place and considered a sacred mountain to the Yavapai people. Located in north central Arizona in the Black Hills mountain range within the Prescott National Forest, Mingus Mountain is traversed by State Route 89A, approximately midway between the cities of Cottonwood and Prescott. Montezuma Castle National Monument
The Montezuma Castle National Monument is a culturally significant place to the Yavapai and Apache people and others including the Hopi. The monument is a 15-20 room ancestral pueblo cliff ruin 100 feet above beaver creek which was once a riparian oasis. Early visitors to the monument were allowed access to the structure by climbing a series of ladders up the side of the limestone cliffs. However, due to extensive damage to this valuable cultural landmark, public access of the ruins was discontinued in 1951. Very few original artifacts remain due to intensive looting of the site. Well over 10,000 ancestors once lived in small villages along the Beaver Creek and nearby tributaries of the Verde River. The monument is adjacent to the Yavapai Apache Nation reservation and the nation’s Cliff Castle Casino, located of I-17 in the Verde Valley just over 100 miles north of Phoenix, AZ. Montezuma Well National Monument
The Montezuma Well National Monument is a culturally significant place and considered a sacred well by the Yavapai and Apache people and others including the Hopi. According to Yavapai oral story, this is a place of Yavapai emergence from the previous underworld. In and around the well are remnants of the ancestors old villages (ruins) and a canal network which is still utilized today. The Monument, a detached unit of Montezuma Castle National Monument, is a natural limestone sinkhole near the town of Rimrock, Arizona through which some 1.5 million gallons of water emerge each day from an underground spring pouring into Beaver Creek. At nearly 400 feet deep, the well is located about 11 miles northeast of Montezuma Castle in the Verde Valley just over 100 miles north of Phoenix, AZ.  Read more… and more…

Oak Flats Campground

Phoenix Indian Boarding School
The Phoenix Indian Boarding School is a historically significant place to many Indigenous Peoples whose grandparents attended. Once Native Americans were confined to reservations in the 1880s, the federal government embarked on a plan to bring about their disappearance–not by military means, but by assimilating their children through education. Owen Lindauer who studied the Archaeology of the Phoenix Indian School,  yielded subtle archaeological evidence that–along with early records of the school (including its newspaper), biographical accounts of employees and students, and historical records of school life–documents the students’ reactions to this attempt to suppress their tribal traditions and identities. The evidence suggests that the need to get along with Indians of different tribes as well as non-Indians, the knowledge that the federal government treated them unlike other people, and the alienation some felt when they returned home fostered a new, pan-Indian identity. Unbelievably, according to the Institute’s archives, the Indian Commissioner at that time said: “It’s cheaper to educate Indians than to kill them.” 

Reclaiming the land and making new memories, the Phoenix Indian Center and Native American Connections signed a Memorandum of Understanding with the City of Phoenix in 2014 to renovate the band building at what is now referred to as Steele Indian School Park. Where it will become a place of celebrating the cultures instead of assimilation of the 23 Indigenous nations that attended. Read more… and more…

Pueblo Grande Museum & Archaeological Park
The Pueblo Grande Museum & Archaeological Park is a culturally significant place to the O’odham people. The museum is located on a 1,500 year old archaeological site left by the Hohokam culture, the ancestors of the O’odham, just minutes from downtown Phoenix next to Sky Harbor International Airport. It is the largest Hohokam archaeological village site within Phoenix, Arizona and includes a partially excavated platform mound, ballcourt, and replicated prehistoric houses. The Hohokam ancestors developed an intricate network of canals consisting of hundreds of miles that spanned across the Salt and Gila River Valleys. These same canals are still being used today by modern day municipalities. Read more… and more…

San Francisco Peaks
The San Francisco Peaks is a culturally significant place and considered a sacred mountain to over 13 Indigenous Nations. Throughout northern Arizona, the horizon is dramatically marked by three 12,000-foot volcanic peaks that rise out of the desert Colorado Plateau south of the Grand Canyon and north of Flagstaff, Arizona. The mountain has the highest elevation in Arizona reaching 12,633 feet at its summit and holds an important place in the oral stories of local Indigenous Peoples. For example, the Hopi believe the mountain is the home of their many Kachinas spirits who live among the clouds around the summit. The sacred mountain is threatened by economic development where treated wastewater is being used to create artificial snow for Arizona Snowbowl, a popular skiing and snowboarding destination. Read more…

Spur Cross Ranch Conservation
The Spur Cross Ranch Conservation area is a culturally significant place to the Yavapai, O’odham and other local Indigenous Peoples. Nearly 90 archaeological sites in the park, including ancestral ruins and what is believed to be defensive forts, are evidence of early human existence in the area. Spur Cross Ranch conserves both desert valley floor and spectacular rock outcroppings rising almost 4,000 feet into the mesas above. One of the last remaining year-round spring-fed streams in Cave Creek, Arizona (30 minutes north of Phoenix) flows through Spur Cross. Its banks are covered with plants, cactus and trees, including mesquite, cottonwoods, willows, and saguaro. Abundant water and plant life make this a home to many species of animals including over 80 species of birds, javelina, mule deer, and coyotes. Read more…

Sunset Crater Volcano National Monument
The Sunset Crater National Monument is a culturally significant place to the Hopi, Navajo and other nearby Indigenous Peoples. Roughly 900 years ago, the eruption of this volcano reshaped the surrounding landscape, forever changing the lives of people, plants and animals. Sunset Crater is a cinder cone located just north of Flagstaff, Arizona and is the youngest in a string of over 600 volcanoes that are related to the nearby San Francisco Peaks. The volcano eruption was witnessed by nearby ancestral villages that were occupied during the time of eruption. Read more…

Tonto National Monument
The Tonto National Monument and surrounding Tonto Basin area is a culturally significant place to the Apache and other Indigenous Peoples such as the Hopi, Yavapai and O’odham. The monument preserves two pueblo style cliff dwellings or ancestral villages 7-8 stories high that overlook the Salt River Tonto Basin below. The river provided a year-round source of water for drinking, agriculture and religious activities. Both ancestral villages have large water storage collection tanks that were used to harvest rain water. The surrounding Tonto Basin is a place of over 100 archaeological sites (much of it now covered by Roosevelt Lake) which was home to a blended culture and unique and vibrant society, influenced from the desert Hohokam people in the south and west and the ancestral Puebloan of the mountain areas to the north and east. Tonto National Monument is located in the Superstition Mountains, in Gila County of central Arizona, approximately 100 miles west of Phoenix. Tuba City Trading Post
The Tuba City Trading Post is a historically significant place to the Hopi and Navajo people and others including the Paiute. Beginning in the 1870’s and officially established in 1906, the post hasn’t changed much and continues to be a place of commerce for local Indigenous Peoples in Northern Arizona. Located on the Navajo Nation reservation, the population of Tuba City is primarily Navajo; however, the Hopi villages of Moenkopi are located just south of town, in addition, the small landless San Juan Southern Paiute Tribe is headquartered in Tuba City. The written history of the town dates back more than 200 years from Spanish missionaries. Read more…

Tuzigoot National Monument
The Tuzigoot National Monument is a culturally significant place to the Yavapai, Apache, Hopi and other Indigenous Peoples. Preserved at the monument is a 2-3 story pueblo ancestral village (ruin) on the summit of a limestone and standstone ridge 120 feet above the Verde River floodplain. The central rooms stand higher than the others, appearing to have served public functions. The old pueblo has approximately 110 rooms and is the largest and best-preserved of the many pueblo ruins found in the Verde Valley. Tuzigoot is located near Clarkdale, Arizona, just over 100 miles north of Phoenix. Read more…

Walnut Canyon National Monument
The Walnut Canyon National Monument is a culturally significant place to the Hopi and other Indigenous Peoples. The monument preserves over 80 pueblo cliff dwellings or ancestral ruins on approximately 3,600 acres. Carved by Walnut Creek, the 400 foot deep canyon has an abundant mix of plants and animals drawn here by water and varied topography. Water flowed intermittently on the canyon floor, providing the lifeblood of the community. Shaded pools in the bottom held precious water between rains. Walnut Canyon is located 7.5 miles east of Flagstaff, Arizona. Read more…

Waputki National Monument
The Waputki National Monument is considered a sacred place to the Hopi people and is a culturally significant place to many other Indigenous Peoples. Nestled in between the Painted Desert and a ponderosa forest, the thousands of archaeological sites, including large and small villages, at Waputki show evidence of human existence in the area for well over 10,000 years. The old villages combined to create a major social and religious center with trade networks expanded bringing exotic items like turquoise, shell jewelry, copper bells, and parrots. The area served has a major meeting point along the Little Colorado River. Waputki National Monument borders the Navajo Nation reservation and is located 50 miles north of Flagstaff, Arizona. Read more…

White Tank Mountain Regional Park
The White Tank Mountain Regional Park is culturally significant and considered a sacred mountain to the O’odham people and others including the Yavapai and Hopi. The White Tank Mountain and its canyons, which make up the park, are home to eleven known archaeological sites attributed to the Hohokam ancestors. These old ancestral villages appear to have been occupied for long periods by sizeable populations. The mountains likely held water year-round and thereby drew people to the area well over 10,000 years ago. “Petroglyph Plaza” is the largest group of rock art panels inside the park with hundreds of figures and symbols leading up to a sacred waterfall. The park is located 30 miles west of Phoenix, Arizona. Read more…

McCloud River
The McCloud River in Northern California snakes its way through the scenic canyons 14,000 feet below Mt. Shasta’s summit. The entire area, including the McCloud River is significant to culture and spirituality of the the Shasta, Wintu, Achomawi, Modoc, Karuk, and other tribes. The Winnemem Wintu Tribe are currently seeking federal recognition and protection of their coming of age ceremonies for young women, which they conduct along the McCloud River’s edge, from public disruption for short periods in the summer.

Mount Diablo
Mount Diablo is significant to many California Native American peoples; according to Miwok and Ohlone, it was the point of creation. Large groups from hundreds of villages would meet in this area for feasts and dances. Mount Diablo is a focal point of their tribal spirituality, with traditions and creation accounts that featured the mountain which also served as a place setting for tribal moral and ceremonial stories. Prior to European entry, the creation narrative varied among surrounding local groups. In one surviving narrative fragment, Mount Diablo and Reed’s Peak (Mount Tamalpais) were surrounded by water; from these two islands the creator Coyote and his assistant Eagle-man made Indian people and the world. In another, Molok the Condor brought forth his grandson Wek-Wek the Falcon Hero, from within the mountain. About 25 independent tribal groups with well-defined territories lived in the East Bay countryside surrounding the mountain. Their members spoke dialects of three distinct languages: Ohlone, Bay Miwok, and Northern Valley Yokuts. Read more…

Mount Shasta
Mount Shasta stands at 14,161 feet, it is the 2nd highest volcano in the Cascade range, after Mt. Rainer and is significant to several tribes including the Shasta, Quartz Valley Indian Rancheria, Achomawi, Ajumawi, Atsugewi, Big Valley Band of Pomo Indians, Hammawi, Karuk, Klamath, Modoc,  Okwanuchu, Wintu, Yana Tribes and many more. The area had substantial human habitation in the surrounding area, with artifacts in the greater region suggesting more than 9,000 years of Native American habitation. Many of these tribes and descendants continue to go there to pray and gather medicinal plants and serves as a place setting to retell and remember identity and cultural strengthening moral and ceremonial stories. The mountain was revered as a place of great medicine and spiritual travel, with some tribes not going beyond the timber line at 8,000 – 8,500 feet.  

The lore of some of the Klamath Tribes in the area held that Mount Shasta is inhabited by the Spirit of the Above-World, Skell, who descended from heaven to the mountain’s summit at the request of a Klamath chief. Skell fought with Spirit of the Below-World, Llao, who resided at Mount Mazama by throwing hot rocks and lava, probably representing the volcanic eruptions at both mountains. Read more…

Soldier Mountain
Soldier Mountain is culturally significant and considered a sacred mountain to the eleven bands of the Pit River Indian Tribe. It is a place of power and great reverence in local Indigenous origin stories. Atop the peak a pitted or hollowed stone boulder is believed to be found. Located in northern California twenty miles north of the Pit River reservation, Soldier Mountain stands around 5,500 feet. The mountain is adjacent to another culturally significant place, the Ahjumawi Lava Springs State Park.  “Where the waters come together….” is a translation of the word Ahjumawi, which is also the self describing word used by the band of Pit River people who inhabited the area. The waters which come together are Big Lake, Tule River, Ja-She Creek, Lava Creek, and Fall River. Together they form one of the largest systems of freshwater springs in the country. Read more…

Hesperus Mountain
Hesperus Mountain is culturally significant and considered a sacred mountain to many Indigenous Peoples including the Navajo, Apache, Ute, the Northern Pueblo people and others. The mountain is known to the Navajo people as the Sacred Mountain of the North, the Big Mountain Sheep or Obsidian Mountain. The mountain is considered to be the northern boundary of Dine Bikeyah, the Navajo traditional homeland marked by four sacred cardinal mountains. The tallest peak sits over 13,200 feet in elevation and is a visually prominent landmark in the surrounding area. Located near the city of Durango, Colorado, the summit of Hesperus is accessible by taking a 48 mile scenic drive from downtown Durango. Read more…

Mount Blanca
Mount Blanca is culturally significant and considered a sacred mountain to many Indigenous Peoples including the Navajo, Apache, Ute, Comanche, the northern Pueblo people and others. Mount Blanca, or Blanca Peak, is known to the Navajo people as the Sacred Mountain of the East, the Dawn or White Shell Mountain. The mountain is considered to be the eastern boundary of Dine Bikeyah, the traditional Navajo homeland marked by four sacred cardinal mountains. Towering over 14,300 feet, Blanca Peak is the 4th highest peak in Colorado. The summit is the entire drainage basin of the Rio Grande River and is higher than any point in the United States east of its longitude. It lies at the southern tip of Colorado’s Sangre de Cristo range, 25 miles east of Alamosa, and it borders another culturally significant place, the Great Sand Dunes National Monument. Read more… and more…

Cottonwood Butte
Cottonwood Butte is culturally significant and a sacred place to the Nez Perce Tribe who call themselves Niimíipuu – “The People”. Located south of the Nez Perce reservation, the butte and surrounding landscape is important in the creation stories of the Niimíipuu and was a special place where the people went to seek the spirit who helped guide them through life. The town of Cottonwood, Idaho is 5 miles east and was the site of the 1877 Cottonwood Skirmish between the U.S Calvary and Nez Perce warriors. The warriors, escaping the advancement of the Calvary, surprised and killed a detachment of 10 men. This is one of many battles and documented sites from the Nez Perce War of 1877. Read more…

Pilot Knob
The Pilot Knob forested area is culturally significant and considered a sacred place to the Nez Perce Tribe. The people of Nez Perce call themselves Niimíipuu which means “The People”. Overlooking the Niimíipuu traditional homeland at over 7,100 feet in the Nez Perce-Clearwater National Forests, Pilot Knob is located directly southeast of the Nez Perce reservation in north central Idaho. The area contains old ancestral cultural sites and is part of the historic Nez Perce trail which was used long before the time of mountain men and miners. Pilot Rock is a place of worship where ancestors and now modern Niimíipuu go to seek visions from the higher power. The Nez Perce Tribe is presently negotiating with the Forest Service to establish an undisturbed area around this inimitable sacred place. Read more…

Seven Devils Mountain
Seven Devils Mountain holds various legends told over the generations of Nez Perce people whose current reservation is located 90 miles north in north central Idaho. The mountain and landscape is culturally significant and considered a sacred place to the Nez Perce. One legend describes a lost man in the area who had a vision of seven dancing devils; another legend details how coyote turned seven giant monsters into mountains to save the people. Seven nearby mountain peaks climb skyward from the Snake River to over 9,400 feet, and bear names like She Devil, He Devil, Ogre, Goblin, Devil’s Throne, Mt. Belial and Twin Imps. The surrounding area is known as the Hell’s Canyon Wilderness and is carved from the powerful waters of the Snake River which separate the states of Oregon and Idaho. Heavens Gate lookout offers an incredible view of portions of Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and Montana. Read more…

Chief Mountain
Ninastako or Chief Mountain is a living landscape significant to the Kainai, Pikuni, Piegan and Siksika Tribes. These tribes create the Blackfoot Nation, speaking the same language, and practicing the same spirituality. The Blackfoot culture is intimately tied to significant places in their traditional territory that spans from Red Deer in Alberta Canada, along the Rocky Mountain front, to the Yellowstone River, in Wyoming. The mountain is revered, remembered and still used by the Niitsiitapi (the people) through stories, teachings, and place of prayer. Many stories still told today pass down information on how cultural items came to the Niitsiitapi, creation of the world, and assignment of languages to the worlds different beings. Ninastako is located in Montana on the eastern border of Glacier National Park and the western border of the Blackfeet Indian Reservation. Medicine Tree
The Medicine Tree near Conner Montana is a culturally and spiritually significant place to the Confederated Salish and Kootenai Tribes and the Nez Perce people. The tree is nearly 400 years old and in its early years it witnessed a fight between coyote and a monstrous ram who had been terrorizing the valley. Outwitting the ram, coyote convinced him to charge, tricking him into hitting the tree instead, where his horn remained embedded 5 feet above the roots. Coyote declared the trees location a space of peace and love, where travelers still pay homage with offerings and prayers for good luck. In 2001, the tree toppled, and vandals have desecrated the sight, but the people continue to return and the trees significance continues for Indigenous people and many Montanans growing up near it.

First Peoples Buffalo Jump State Park
The First Peoples Buffalo Jump State Park is a significant location to many plains tribes, primarily the Blackfoot Nation Tribes. The mile long cliff with nearly 18ft of compacted bison bones was used primarily by the Blackfeet Tribe, the Southern tribe of the Blackfoot Nations with most recent archaeological evidence dating back to 500 A.D. First Peoples Buffalo Jump State Park, is a National Historic Landmark and an expansive archaeological site of the largest bison cliff jump in North America. This area was the primary food source for the Blackfeet, where evidence of camping and cooking can be seen at the Vivendi Site.  Read more… and more…

Giant Springs
Only 28 minutes East of the First Peoples Buffalo Jump is a series of waterfalls, Black Eagle, Colter, Rainbow, Crooked and the Great Falls, that give the City of Great Falls, Montana its name. Giant Springs contribute 156 million gallons a day of 3000 year old water into the Missouri River. The nearby 2500 year old archaeology site called the Vivendi Site shows proof of extensive camping, and cooking by the ancestors of the Blackfoot and Mandan. Read more…

Sweet Grass Hills
Located in Northern Montana minutes from the Canadian border are the Sweet Grass Hills, a culturally and spiritually significant location to the Blackfoot, Cheyenne, and Salish people. The three distinctive buttes on the horizon east of the Rocky Mountains are called collectively the Sweet Grass Hills. West Butte, the closest and highest, rises 3,000 feet above the plains. Middle Butte, farther in the distance and a little to the south, is grass covered and East Butte is the most distant. Together these three buttes make this range the most prominent in north central Montana. Read more…

Mount Taylor
Mount Taylor is culturally significant and considered a sacred mountain to many Indigenous Peoples including the Pueblos of Acoma, Laguna and Zuni, and other nations such as the Hopi, Navajo and Apache. The mountain is known to the Navajo people as the Sacred Mountain of the South, the Blue Bead or Turquoise Mountain. The mountain is considered to be the southern boundary of Dine Bikeyah, the Navajo traditional homeland marked by four sacred cardinal mountains. Repeated eruptions left a great volume of volcanic debris around the area and some suggest the mountain might once have stood 25,000 feet in elevation. Five local Indigenous nations successfully and collectively pushed for the New Mexico Supreme Court to rule Mount Taylor as “traditional cultural property” which helps provide control and better protection of the environment and archaeological, historical and cultural sites against the devastating exploitative mining companies. The summit of Mount Taylor may be reached by truck in a short 23 mile drive from Grants, New Mexico. Read more…

Top of page

Crater Lake National Park
Crater Lake National Park is culturally significant and considered a sacred place to the Klamath and Umpqua people along with many other Indigenous Peoples. 7,700 years ago, the people witnessed the formation of the lake when a violent eruption, lasting only two days, triggered the collapse of a towering volcano. Archaeologist have found sandals and other artifacts buried under layers of ash, dust, and pumic from this eruption. Fed by rain and snow, Crater Lake is the deepest lake in the U.S at nearly 2,000 feet deep and is suggested to be one of the purist water bodies on earth. Many legends and oral stories are associated with the lake and even farther back before when it was once a prominent mountain among the Cascade Range. Crater Lake is located in southern Oregon in between the reservations of the Cow Creek Band of Umpua Tribe of Indians and The Klamath Tribes. Read more…

Mount Hood
Mount Hood is culturally significant and considered a sacred mountain to many Indigenous nations including the Confederated Tribes of Siletz Indians, the Confederated Tribes of Grand Ronde, the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs, the Confederated Tribes and Bands of the Yakama Nation and others. Standing at around 11,240 feet, it is the tallest mountain in Oregon located 20 miles east of the city of Portland and is considered the most likely to erupt in the state. Local origin stories from Indigenous people speak of the mountains active volcanic history. This history is embedded in the cultural belief and cosmology of the local Indigenous people. Serving as a prominent landmark, it is visible up to 100 miles away. Read more…

Wallowa Lake
Wallowa Lake is culturally significant and considered a sacred place to the Nez Perce Tribe. The northern shore of the lake is the resting place of Old Chief Joseph who died around 1870 and was the father of the great Chief Joseph who was a prominent leader among the Nez Perce in the late 1870’s who fought for his people’s survival. In the Nez Perce War of 1877, Chief Joseph and the Nez Perce people resisted takeover of their lands from the U.S. but eventually were forced to hideout in the mountains and flee Oregon in an attempt to reach Canada and avoid being corralled onto a reservation. They were pursued by the U.S. Calvary and tracked down as they approached the Canadian border, pushing Chief Joseph to surrender. In 1886, the Old Chief Joseph gravesite was sadly desecrated by local property owners and his skull was inhumanely removed as a souvenir. In 1926, 2,500 people lined up to see the remains of tiwi-teqis or Old Chief Joseph repatriated at a new gravesite at the base of Lake Wallowa, overlooking the lands he once called home. Located in northeast Oregon, outside the town of Joseph, the lake is part of an integral landscape of the Nimiipuu or Nez Perce homeland. Read more…

  • Wallowa Lake is known to the Nez Perce as “Wallowa” which, when translated, means “Winding Water”.

Top of page

The Black Hills
The Black Hills is considered a sacred place to many Indigenous nations including the Cheyenne, Arapaho, Crow, Three Affiliated Tribes, Sioux, Pawnee, Kiowa and others. The Black Hills are a small isolated mountain range rising over 7,000 feet from the Great Plains of North America in western South Dakota and northeastern Wyoming. Approximately 110 miles long and 70 miles wide, consisting of 1.2 million acres of forested hills and mountains, the Black Hills are described as an “Island in the Plains”. Indigenous Peoples, like their ancestors for thousands of years, still today visit the Black Hills for physical and spiritual renewal. The mountains are called the Black Hills because when seen from a distance, these pine-covered hills, rising several thousand feet above the surrounding prairie, appear black. The Treaty of Fort Laramie in 1868 secured the Black Hills from white settlement forever; but unfortunately, just six years later, gold was discovered. Read more…

Significant Places in Washington

Mount Adams ♦ Mount St. Helens

Top of page

Mount Adams
Mount Adams is culturally significant and considered a sacred mountain to many Indigenous nations including the Confederated Tribes and Bands of the Yakama Nation, the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs, Cowlitz and others. A depiction of the sacred mountain is on the Great Seal of the Yakama Nation and the eastern side of the mountain lies on their reservation. Believed to have last erupted over 1,000 years ago, Mount Adams is potentially an active stratovolano and towers about 9,800 feet above the surrounding countryside making it the third largest in the Cascade Range. Mount Adams and the other mountains in the Cascade Range hold great importance in the origin stories of the local Indigenous Peoples throughout the Pacific Northwest. This sacred mountain is located 50 miles west of Yakima, Washington. Read more…

Mount St. Helens
Mount St. Helens is culturally significant and considered a sacred mountain to many Indigenous nations including the Confederated Tribes and Bands of the Yakama Nation, the Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs, the Confederated Tribes of Chehalis, Cowlitz and others. Known as a sibling volcano to Mount Adams which is just 20 miles or so west, Mount St. Helens is the most active stratovolcano in the Cascade Range and holds great importance in the origin stories of the local Indigenous people. Mount St. Helens is located 96 miles south of Seattle, Washington, and last erupted in 1980 resulting in the deadliest and most economically destructive volcano in U.S. history. The eruption shaved over 1,300 feet off the summit, replacing it with a 1 mile wide horseshoe shape crater. The Yakama people and others have witnessed the mountains activity likely since its young beginning since it formed within the last 40,000 years. Read more…
